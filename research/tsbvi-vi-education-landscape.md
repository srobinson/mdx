## Executive Summary

TSBVI is a small but symbolically important institution: 129 on-campus students (2024-25 NCES data), a 40+ acre campus in Austin, and an ~$49.5M biennial all-funds budget (2022-23 LBB data; roughly $25M/year). The $1.35M assistive technology budget figure cited in the pitch is not substantiated by any public document and is almost certainly an inflated or misattributed number. The pitch's 55,000+ VI student claim is technically accurate as a census of legally blind students in US educational settings, but 85% of those students are mainstreamed in public schools, and only ~8% (approximately 4,400) attend residential schools -- the natural first market for a campus navigation product. The O&M specialist shortage is genuine and well-documented, but O&M instruction emphasizes skill-building over tool dependency, which creates adoption friction. Grant funding through AFB and TEA exists but at modest scale; AFB does not operate a direct grant program for external technology vendors.

## Detailed Findings

### 1. TSBVI Campus and Enrollment

**Campus size:** Over 40 acres in north-central Austin, bounded by Burnet (west), Lamar (east), W 45th Street (south), and W 49th Street (north). The campus was once 73 acres; it has contracted over its 150+ year history. The footprint is substantial enough that outdoor navigation between buildings is a genuine practical challenge.

**On-campus enrollment:** 129 students for 2024-25 per NCES. This is consistent with the statutory cap of 150 boarding students. The school confirms a residential program capacity at or near 150. Historical data from 2008 noted 50% of students were high school age; current NCES data shows the largest cohort in Grade 12 (59 of 129 students).

**Residential vs. day student breakdown:** The Annual Improvement Plan explicitly references "an increasing number of day students" and notes the school created a Day Student Coordinator role specifically to address their Independent Living and Recreation/Leisure needs. No precise numerical breakdown is published, but the plan language suggests residential students remain the majority while day attendance is growing. The statutory cap is 150 boarding students; day students are served in addition.

**Total served statewide:** TSBVI's outreach programs serve "on average 11,000 students" across Texas who are blind, visually impaired, or deafblind. As of the 2025 VI Registry, Texas has 10,907 identified VI students: 8,528 in special education and 2,379 on 504 plans. Of those, 3,146 meet the legal definition of blindness.

**Staff:** 465 budgeted positions for FY2024 (282 FT, 183 PT). The 2022-23 LBB document shows 365.2 FTE cap.

**Key leadership for technology decisions:**
- Superintendent: Emily Coleman (512-206-9133)
- Director of Information Resources: Scott Brackett (512-206-9210)
- Outreach AT Consultant: Carrie Gillespie (runs the Tech Loan Program)
- O&M Outreach Consultant: Scott Turner

Technology decisions sit primarily with Scott Brackett (Information Resources) for infrastructure and Carrie Gillespie for assistive technology. The Annual Improvement Plan references the Information Resources department under direct Superintendent oversight.

### 2. TSBVI Budget and the $1.35M Claim

**Total budget (2022-23 biennium, LBB Agency 771):**
- General Revenue: $33.4M
- Federal Funds: $4.4M
- Other Funds (interagency contracts): $11.7M
- **All Funds Total: $49.5M biennial** (~$24.7M/year)

**Program-level breakdown (2022-23 biennial):**
- Classroom Instruction (A.1.1): $12.6M
- Residential Program (A.1.2): $7.4M
- Short-Term Programs (A.1.3): $1.9M
- Related and Support Services (A.1.4): $12.3M
- Technical Assistance / Outreach (B.1.1): $4.6M
- Professional Education in VI (B.1.2): $2.8M
- Central Administration: $4.8M
- Other Support Services: $2.1M

For 2024-25, the LBB recommended compensation increases of $1.0M/year to match AISD educator salaries, and the General Appropriations Act for 2024-25 (88th Legislature) was enacted. Total budget likely remains in the $25-27M annual range, adjusted for inflation and salary increases.

**The $1.35M assistive technology budget claim:** No public TSBVI document, LBB summary, or TEA grant record references a $1.35M figure for assistive technology. The entire "Related and Support Services" strategy (A.1.4) runs $12.3M biennially ($6.2M/year) and covers all therapy, evaluation, and support functions, not just AT. The most plausible interpretation is that a pitch author extrapolated or fabricated this number. The actual AT program (the Tech Loan Program operated by Carrie Gillespie) started with a one-time Hobletzelle Foundation grant plus federal funds; no recurring budget line of $1.35M is documented. The comparable AFB grant to the Virginia Murray Sowell Center for AT teacher training was $1.25M over five years -- which may be the source of confusion.

**Per-student cost:** The LBB has previously cited $55,000/student when counting only on-campus students, $32,000 for residential programs, and $25,000 for day programs. When amortized across all 11,000 statewide recipients, cost drops to approximately $8,000/student. These are the most current publicly sourced figures (cited in an LBB issue brief).

### 3. O&M Instruction: Constraints, Shortage, and Tool Adoption

**What O&M is:** Orientation and Mobility specialists teach safe, independent navigation through the environment. Core skills include long cane technique, systematic search patterns, street crossings, public transit, and increasingly GPS/app-based navigation. All O&M instruction is one-on-one by definition.

**Shortage:** The O&M specialist shortage is real and well-documented. According to TSBVI's own 2024-25 Annual Improvement Plan (Outreach section):
- Texas had 347 COMS (Certified Orientation and Mobility Specialists) providing direct service in 2023, equaling 276 FTEs.
- The total increased by 42 individuals in 2023, but 39 of those are part-time.
- TSBVI projects Texas will need 26 to 36 additional COMS by 2026 for student growth alone.
- Texas needs 75 to 85 additional FTE COMS in 3 years to replace projected retirees and handle growth.
- 49 of 49 COMS left the field in 2023 were only partially replaced.

**Nationally:** Approximately 28,000 professionals are needed to serve blind and VI children in schools. Individual state data varies widely: Alabama had 29 COMS serving 1,660 eligible students (~57 students/COMS); California recommends a cap of 12 students per specialist.

**How O&M specialists view technology tools:** O&M training at the graduate level increasingly emphasizes technology integration. Current tools used in O&M sessions include BlindSquare (GPS), smartphone wayfinding apps (Apple Maps, Google Maps with VoiceOver/TalkBack), Aira (remote sighted assistance), and Be My Eyes. Seeing AI's indoor navigation feature has been positively reviewed by Perkins School for the Blind. However, the O&M professional community is cautious about tools that substitute for learned skills. A common principle: technology augments but should not replace internalized spatial mapping. This creates a nuanced adoption dynamic -- O&M specialists will embrace tools that extend training, but resist tools that create dependence.

**At TSBVI specifically:** The Annual Improvement Plan reports 96% of on-campus students making moderate-to-substantial progress in O&M (measured by TSBVI's own performance indicators). TSBVI has 2 O&M teachers collaborating with local education agencies (LEAs) during the transition process. O&M is listed as a strength, not a need area, in the 2024-25 plan.

### 4. TSBVI Technology Adoption Profile

**Current technology infrastructure:**
- GEER (Governor's Emergency Education Relief) grant funds were used to refresh laptops and iPads.
- TSBVI adopted Asset Tiger for device tracking.
- WiFi access point installation is an active 2024-25 infrastructure goal.
- Assistive Technology is a formal course in the Career and Technology Education curriculum.
- The school's AT program achieved 96% of students making moderate-to-substantial progress.

**Tech Loan Program:** TSBVI's Outreach department operates a statewide technology loan library of braillewriters, screen readers, braille printers, communication devices, low vision aids, and other AT. Loans go to school districts, not directly to students. The program is run by Carrie Gillespie.

**Receptivity to external partnerships:** The Annual Improvement Plan cites partnerships with HEB (work training), Austin Lighthouse for the Blind (internships), Texas Workforce Commission, and technology vendors (an earlier TX SenseAbilities article mentioned collaboration with "Brava" on AT solutions). TSBVI explicitly describes partnership development as a Long-Range Goal (Goal E): "Partnerships will be developed so that students who are blind, deafblind, or have low vision have quality resources available at the TSBVI campus and in their home district."

**Navigation technology on campus:** No public record exists of TSBVI deploying BlindSquare beacons, indoor GPS, or any dedicated campus navigation app. The current standard appears to be O&M instruction combined with smartphone-based tools. BlindSquare beacon deployments at higher education institutions (Columbus State Community College, University of Guelph) were reported in 2018-19, but no K-12 residential school pilot has been found in public records.

**Decision-making process for new AT:** The Tech Loan Program requires an individualized technology evaluation submitted before loans are made. This evaluation documents student abilities, functional vision, present level of functioning, short and long-term technology needs, and a training plan. Any new campus navigation system would require integration into this IEP/evaluation framework.

### 5. VI Student Demographics and Market Sizing

**National landscape (APH Federal Quota Census, 2022 data -- most recent published):**
- 54,962 legally blind students in educational settings below college level registered with APH
- 8% (4,428) registered by residential schools for the blind
- 85% (47,593) registered by state departments of education (mainstreamed)
- 5% (2,676) registered by rehabilitation programs
- 2% (940) registered by multiple disability programs

**IDEA data (NCES, 2022-23):** Visual impairments accounted for 2% or less of students served under IDEA. The Statista figure for ages 3-21 with visual impairments covered by IDEA was approximately 25,000 in 2021-22 -- significantly lower than the APH count of 54,962. This discrepancy arises because APH registration includes students with other primary disabilities who also have visual impairments, while IDEA counts students by primary disability. The two datasets measure different populations.

**The "55,000+ VI students" pitch claim:** The number is defensible as a citation of the APH census (54,962 rounded up). However, using this as the total addressable market for a campus navigation app is a significant misrepresentation:
- 85% are in mainstream public schools with no specialized campus infrastructure
- A meaningful portion have additional disabilities that complicate navigation app use (30% are symbolic or non-readers; 18% are pre-readers per APH data)
- Only ~4,428 are in residential schools that have the controlled campus environment suited to a campus navigation product
- Residential schools for the blind in the US number approximately 50 to 60 institutions nationally, with widely varying campus layouts and enrollment sizes

**Texas specifically:** 10,907 identified VI students statewide. Of these, 129 attend TSBVI on campus, and an additional ~100-200 may attend other specialized programs. The vast majority receive services from local districts.

**Reading medium context:** Among the 54,962 APH-registered students, only 8% (4,342) use braille as their primary reading medium. 33% use print/large print. This matters for navigation app interface design: most VI students are low-vision, not totally blind, and audio-first interfaces should not be assumed universal.

### 6. Grant Funding: What Actually Exists

**AFB (American Foundation for the Blind):**
- AFB is primarily an advocacy, research, and policy organization, not a grant-making foundation in the traditional sense.
- It does not operate a technology vendor grant program. The $1.25M grant to the Virginia Murray Sowell Center was an exceptional, one-time award for teacher training infrastructure.
- AFB's Ford Foundation grant (2024) was $150,000 for internal research on digital inclusion.
- AFB offers scholarships to individuals ($2,000-$28,000 depending on education level) but not to companies or schools for technology acquisition.
- **Conclusion:** AFB is not a viable funding source for a navigation app vendor.

**TEA (Texas Education Agency) -- actual programs:**

1. **Special Education Specialized Supports Grant (2024-25):** Awarded to Education Service Center Region 11. Funds dissemination of technical assistance and professional development for AT, deaf/HH, deafblind, and VI programs. Amount not disclosed publicly; the grant went to an ESC, not directly to schools or vendors.

2. **DeafBlind Graduate Certificate Grant Initiative:** TEA funds tuition/fees for qualified candidates pursuing a graduate certificate. Targeted at workforce development, not technology procurement.

3. **Texas Technology Access Program (TTAP):** Operates under the federal Assistive Technology Act (21st Century AT Act), administered by TEA, run by TSBVI. Funding goes toward a loan library, demonstration centers, and reuse/recycle programs. Not a grant program for technology vendors.

4. **IDEA Part B (federal):** TSBVI received approximately $3.2M in IDEA Special Education Grants in 2022-23 and $1.0M in Deaf-Blind Centers funding. These flow to TSBVI directly as a state education agency; they fund direct services, not technology vendor contracts.

5. **APH Federal Quota:** Congress appropriates funds annually (~$305/student as of 2018; current figure likely similar given flat federal appropriations). States receive per-pupil allocations that schools can spend on APH-produced materials. This is a procurement mechanism for APH products, not an open grant for external vendors.

**Perkins School for the Blind (Massachusetts):** Perkins operates grant programs and the Perkins eLearning platform, but is not a grant-making foundation for technology vendors in the traditional sense either.

**Practical grant pathways that do exist:**
- OSEP (Office of Special Education Programs) Parent Training and Information Centers and Technical Assistance programs -- but these fund advocacy infrastructure, not products.
- NSF Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) / Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs, specifically the "Accessibility and Assistive Technology" topic area -- legitimate path for an early-stage tech product, typical Phase I awards of $275,000.
- NIH SBIR/STTR under the National Eye Institute -- another legitimate path.
- State AT Act programs (like TTAP) -- these can help with device loans and demonstrations, giving visibility to new products, but do not fund development.

## Actionable Takeaways

**For evaluating the pitch:**

1. **Reject the $1.35M AT budget claim.** No public document supports it. The total annual TSBVI budget is roughly $25M across all functions; a dedicated AT line of $1.35M would represent 5% of total spending, which is inconsistent with the overall budget architecture. The Tech Loan Program started with a private foundation grant -- it is not a recurring capital-expenditure program.

2. **The 55,000 student TAM is misleading.** The honest near-term addressable market for a campus navigation app is the approximately 4,400 students at residential schools -- a much smaller number. The secondary market (mainstreamed VI students navigating public school campuses) is real but requires a different product and distribution channel.

3. **AFB grants do not work the way the pitch implies.** AFB is an advocacy organization. It does not fund technology vendors directly. The pitch should reference NSF SBIR or NIH NEI SBIR instead if it claims a grant runway.

4. **TEA grants are feasible but indirect.** The right TEA pathway is through Education Service Centers (ESCs) that contract for professional development and AT demonstrations, not through a direct school contract. TSBVI's Outreach team is the gatekeeper for statewide VI AT adoption in Texas.

5. **O&M specialist adoption is a viable but nuanced angle.** O&M specialists are genuinely constrained (caseloads of 30-57 students in shortage states), and tools that extend their reach have appeal. However, the professional community is skill-building-first, not tool-first. A product that O&M specialists can prescribe and supervise as part of an IEP-supported program is more viable than one marketed as an independent navigation solution.

6. **TSBVI is receptive to partnerships in principle.** The Annual Improvement Plan explicitly names partnership development as a Long-Range Goal. The entry path is through Carrie Gillespie (AT) for a Tech Loan library inclusion, or Scott Turner (O&M) for a training integration conversation. The Superintendent (Emily Coleman) owns strategic partnerships but the operational path runs through the Directors.

7. **The campus is a genuine validation site.** 40+ acres is large enough that an outdoor navigation product would have real utility. Students routinely travel between classroom buildings, dorms, vocational sites, and community locations. This is not a contrived use case.
